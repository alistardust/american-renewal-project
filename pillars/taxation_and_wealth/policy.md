# Taxation and Wealth — Policy Rules

## Scope Code: ECO (Economic System)

---

## ECO-AUT — Automation and Labor Displacement

### ECO-AUT-001 — Automation Tax on Labor Displacement
**Status:** MISSING

**Statement:** Companies that replace or materially displace human labor with AI or automation should pay an AI worker tax or automated-labor tax.

**Notes:** Establishes taxation on labor displacement by artificial intelligence or automation systems. Tax should be proportional to the extent of displacement—not merely automation adoption, but actual reduction in human employment or hours. Applies to both AI systems (algorithms, machine learning, large language models) and physical automation (robotics, automated manufacturing). Does not tax automation that augments human productivity without displacing workers. Revenue supports affected workers, retraining, and public goods.

**Implementation Considerations:**
- Measurement: Track headcount reduction, hours reduction, or wage bill reduction correlated with automation adoption
- Exemptions: Automation that creates new positions or increases productivity without displacement
- Phasing: Graduated implementation to allow adjustment and prevent shock
- Gaming prevention: Close loopholes like offshoring automation or contractor reclassification

---

### ECO-AUT-002 — Automation Tax Revenue for Public Benefit
**Status:** MISSING

**Statement:** Revenue from AI or automated-labor taxation should be used to support workers, public goods, and broad-based social benefit rather than private concentration alone.

**Notes:** Directs automation tax revenue to public purposes aligned with addressing displacement effects. Specific uses may include: worker retraining and education programs, transition support for displaced workers, universal basic income or social dividend programs, infrastructure investment, research and development, healthcare and social services expansion. Prevents revenue from being captured by general budget where it might subsidize further concentration. Creates direct connection between productivity gains from automation and public benefit.

---

### ECO-AUT-003 — Structure Tax to Discourage Irresponsible Displacement
**Status:** MISSING

**Statement:** AI or automated-labor taxation should be structured to discourage labor displacement without social responsibility and to share productivity gains broadly.

**Notes:** Tax design should create incentives for responsible automation practices. Companies that invest in worker retraining, create new positions, share productivity gains through higher wages, or gradually transition workers rather than abruptly eliminating positions should face lower tax burden. Companies that maximize short-term profit through mass layoffs and automation should face higher burden. Tax structure should encourage augmentation (making workers more productive) over replacement (eliminating workers). Recognizes that some automation is inevitable and beneficial but should not be subsidized or incentivized when it harms workers and communities without offsetting social benefit.

## ECO-EQT — Economic Equality and Opportunity

### ECO-EQT-001 — Equal Pay and Economic Opportunity
**Status:** MISSING

**Statement:** Guarantee equal pay and equal economic opportunity regardless of protected class.

**Notes:** Extends beyond "equal pay for equal work" to address structural barriers to economic opportunity. Requires not only non-discrimination in compensation but also in hiring, promotion, training, networking access, and opportunity. Addresses both explicit discrimination and structural disadvantages that create de facto discrimination. Protected classes include race, gender, sexuality, religion, disability, age, national origin, and other characteristics protected by law. Enforcement must address systemic patterns, not only individual complaints.

**Implementation Considerations:**
- Requires affirmative measures to counteract historical discrimination and structural barriers
- Regular auditing of pay equity and opportunity across protected classes
- Transparency requirements for compensation and advancement
- Addresses "cultural fit" and "networking" justifications that perpetuate discrimination

## ECO-IND — Industrial Policy and Manufacturing

### ECO-IND-001 — National Manufacturing and Industrial Policy
**Status:** MISSING

**Statement:** Establish national manufacturing and industrial policy to strengthen domestic production and supply chains.

**Notes:** Creates framework for affirmative government support of manufacturing and strategic industries. Recognizes that relying entirely on market forces has led to offshoring of production, supply chain vulnerabilities, loss of industrial capacity, and economic hollowing. Industrial policy should prioritize: domestic manufacturing capacity in strategic sectors (semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, renewable energy, batteries, critical minerals), resilient supply chains not dependent on single sources or adversarial nations, worker training and labor standards, alignment with climate and sustainability goals, coordination between federal research and commercial production, support for innovation and advanced manufacturing techniques.

**Not mercantilism or protectionism:** Goal is resilient productive capacity, not autarky. International trade and cooperation remain important, but critical dependencies must be diversified and domestic capacity must be maintained.

**Sectors of priority:** Clean energy, advanced computing, biotechnology and pharmaceuticals, transportation systems, telecommunications infrastructure, food and agriculture, defense-related production.

## ECO-INS — Insurance Regulation and AI

### ECO-INS-001 — AI Cannot Deny Insurance Without Human Review
**Status:** MISSING

**Statement:** AI systems may not be used to deny restrict or reduce insurance coverage or claims without direct independent human review and accountability.

**Notes:** Prohibits use of artificial intelligence or algorithmic systems to deny, restrict, reduce, or delay insurance coverage or claims without direct review by an independent human decision-maker with genuine authority and accountability. Applies to all insurance types: health, property, auto, disability, life, and others. Human reviewer must have access to full information, time to consider the case, and authority to override algorithmic recommendation. Reviewer cannot be penalized for overriding the algorithm or incentivized to defer to it. Creates individual accountability: if human reviewer merely rubber-stamps algorithm without independent judgment, both reviewer and system are liable.

**Purpose:** Prevents insurance companies from using AI as a denial machine where humans serve as legal cover without exercising real judgment. Addresses documented patterns of algorithmic denial, biased training data, and automation bias where humans defer to computer recommendations.

---

### ECO-INS-002 — Independent Human Judgment Required
**Status:** MISSING

**Statement:** Human reviewers in insurance decisions must exercise independent judgment and may not rely solely on AI-generated recommendations.

**Notes:** Reinforces ECO-INS-001 by specifying that human review must be substantive, not perfunctory. Reviewers must: examine the specific case facts, consider information beyond algorithmic inputs, document reasoning that is independent of AI recommendation, be held accountable for decision quality not processing speed. Organizational structures and incentives must support independent judgment—high caseloads, time pressure, or performance metrics based on agreement with algorithm violate this requirement.

**Enforcement mechanisms:** Regular auditing of human review quality, analysis of override rates (both too low and too high are suspicious), investigation of cases where denial patterns suggest algorithmic rather than independent human decision-making, penalties for companies that create rubber-stamp review processes.

## ECO-LAB — Labor Rights and Protections

### ECO-LAB-004 — Government Support for Small Business Parental Leave
**Status:** MISSING

**Statement:** Government must subsidize or support paid parental leave for small businesses.

**Notes:** Recognizes that mandating paid parental leave can burden small businesses disproportionately. Rather than exempting small businesses (which would leave their workers without protection) or imposing unsustainable costs, government provides subsidy, reimbursement, or direct provision. Models include: insurance fund paid through small payroll tax to which employers contribute and from which leave is paid, direct reimbursement to small businesses for wages paid during parental leave, government provision of leave benefits directly to workers with employer accommodation requirements but not wage payment requirements.

**Threshold considerations:** "Small business" defined by employee count, revenue, or other metric. Subsidy could be graduated—full support below threshold, partial support up to larger threshold, no support above threshold. Prevents paid leave policy from becoming competitive advantage for large corporations over small businesses.

## ECO-SMB — Small Business Support

### ECO-SMB-001 — Small Business Healthcare Coverage Support
**Status:** MISSING

**Statement:** Provide subsidies carveouts or public support to help small businesses comply with healthcare coverage requirements.

**Notes:** Similar logic to ECO-LAB-004 for parental leave. If healthcare coverage is mandated (or strongly incentivized), small businesses need support to comply without being driven out by costs. Approaches include: subsidies for small business purchase of employee health coverage, public option or government-provided healthcare that reduces employer burden, graduated requirements where businesses below certain size have reduced obligations or receive offsetting support, carveouts that exempt very small businesses with alternative mechanisms to ensure workers still receive coverage.

**Goal:** Universal healthcare coverage without destroying small business or creating competitive advantage for large corporations that can absorb compliance costs. Long-term solution is single-payer or universal system where employment and coverage are decoupled, but until that exists, transitional support for small businesses is essential.

## ECO-TAX — Taxation Structure

### ECO-TAX-001 — Anti-Wealth Hoarding
**Status:** PARTIAL

**Statement:** Anti-wealth hoarding.

**Notes:** Constitutional-level principle that extreme wealth concentration may be regulated and taxed to prevent threats to democratic self-governance. Does not specify exact mechanisms or rates (which remain statutory) but establishes authority for: progressive income taxation with high marginal rates on extreme income, wealth taxation on assets above defined thresholds, inheritance and estate taxation to prevent dynastic accumulation, land value taxation (Georgist principle) to capture unearned value, taxation of monopoly rents and unearned income at higher rates than labor income.

**Principle:** Taxation must be progressive. Wealth concentration that threatens democratic equality may be limited. Congress has explicit constitutional authority to tax extreme wealth, inheritance, and concentration.

**Status marked PARTIAL:** Suggests this principle has been articulated and partially accepted but not yet fully formalized or implemented. Likely requires constitutional amendment to establish explicitly, as current constitutional doctrine around taxation is ambiguous on wealth taxes.

**Political framing:** Not "soak the rich" but "prevent oligarchy." Not "punish success" but "maintain democratic equality." Not specific rate-setting but durable structural principle that statutory politics can implement flexibly.

**Implementation mechanisms:** Statutory rate-setting by Congress, enforcement through IRS with adequate funding, closure of loopholes (offshore accounts, shell companies, valuation games, trust structures), international cooperation to prevent capital flight, connection to antitrust policy (monopoly rents should not escape taxation).

## Cross-Pillar Integration Notes

### Connection to Anti-Corruption (Pillar 3)
The ECO pillar addresses economic structure while anti-corruption addresses translation of wealth into political power. Both are necessary: limit wealth concentration (ECO) and block channels through which remaining wealth buys influence (COR). Wealth taxation reduces resources available for political capture. Campaign finance limits prevent existing wealth from dominating politics.

### Connection to Rights and Economic Security (Pillar 5)
The ECO pillar structures the economy; Pillar 5 guarantees individual economic security. ECO creates conditions for broad prosperity (progressive taxation, automation revenue, small business support). Pillar 5 ensures individuals have healthcare, housing, livable wages regardless of economic structure. They work together: structure the economy for fairness, and guarantee security for individuals.

### Connection to Antitrust (Pillar 11)
Wealth concentration and market concentration are mutually reinforcing. Monopolies generate monopoly rents that concentrate wealth. Concentrated wealth funds lobbying and political influence that protects monopolies. Breaking market concentration (antitrust) reduces wealth concentration. Taxing wealth reduces resources for market manipulation. Both pillars attack the same problem from different angles.

### Connection to Checks and Balances (Pillar 13)
The SYS-AI rules in Pillar 13 establish overarching principles for AI in government decision-making. The ECO-INS rules are domain-specific implementation for insurance. Both prohibit AI-driven harm without human accountability. The system-level rules ensure consistency across domains; the economic rules provide specific enforcement in insurance and economic decisions.
