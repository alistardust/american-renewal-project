# Antitrust and Corporate Power — Policy Rules

## Scope Codes: COR-FIN (Corporate Finance), MED-PRS (Media/Press - overlapping with Pillar 10)

**Note:** This pillar synthesizes antitrust and corporate power reforms from multiple sources. The COR-FIN rules are formally in the database and represent the corporate political finance component. The MED-PRS rules also appear in Pillar 10 (Information and Media) and are relevant here for media consolidation concerns. Additional antitrust reforms discussed in source materials are included as prose guidance pending formalization into structured rules.

---

## COR-FIN — Corporate Political Finance

### COR-FIN-001 — Ban Corporate Political Donations
**Status:** INCLUDED

**Statement:** Ban corporate political donations.

**Notes:** Prohibits corporations from making political donations to candidates, parties, or political organizations. Closes the channel through which concentrated economic power directly translates into political influence. Applies to all corporate entities regardless of size, industry, or structure. Does not restrict individual donations by corporate officers or employees acting in personal capacity.

---

### COR-FIN-002 — Ban Super PACs
**Status:** PARTIAL

**Statement:** Ban Super PACs.

**Notes:** Eliminates Super PACs and similar independent expenditure organizations that allow unlimited donations for political spending. Super PACs enable end-runs around campaign contribution limits and allow wealthy donors to dominate political messaging. Banning them restores some proportionality between money and political influence. Status marked PARTIAL suggests this rule has been discussed or drafted but not yet fully implemented in formal policy.

---

### COR-FIN-003 — Limit Individual Donations
**Status:** MISSING

**Statement:** Limit individual donations.

**Notes:** Establishes reasonable limits on individual contributions to political campaigns, parties, and committees. Prevents wealthy individuals from using personal wealth to dominate political funding even when corporate and PAC donations are banned. Limits should be high enough to enable meaningful participation but low enough to prevent plutocratic control. Applies per election cycle with inflation adjustment.

---

### COR-FIN-004 — Limit Political Ad Spending
**Status:** MISSING

**Statement:** Limit political ad spending.

**Notes:** Restricts total spending on political advertising to prevent wealthy interests from drowning out other voices through sheer volume of messaging. May include overall spending caps, limits on ad buys in specific media markets, or public financing alternatives. Requires careful constitutional design to survive First Amendment scrutiny while achieving the goal of preventing wealth-based domination of political discourse.

## MED-PRS — Media and Press (Overlap with Pillar 10)

**Context:** These rules formally belong to Pillar 10 (Information and Media) but are relevant to corporate power concerns around media consolidation. They are listed here for cross-reference. Full descriptions appear in Pillar 10 policy document.

### Media Consolidation Concerns
- MED-PRS-001: Protect journalists
- MED-PRS-002: Protect sources
- MED-PRS-003: Limit compelled disclosure
- MED-PRS-004: Ban retaliation against journalists
- MED-PRS-005: Prevent misuse of secrecy laws
- MED-PRS-006: Judicial review before disclosure
- MED-PRS-007: Limit surveillance of journalists
- MED-PRS-008: Include independent journalists
- MED-PRS-009: Transparency on targeting journalists

**Antitrust Relevance:** A free and independent press requires protection from both government interference (Pillar 10 focus) and corporate consolidation that eliminates independent voices. Media ownership concentration creates news deserts, reduces investigative capacity, and allows a few corporations to control information flow. Antitrust enforcement in media must balance free press protection with anti-consolidation imperatives.

## PROSE GUIDANCE — Antitrust Reforms (Source Materials)

**Note:** The following reforms are discussed extensively in source materials but have not yet been formalized into structured rule IDs. They represent the substantive antitrust policy agenda that complements the corporate political finance rules above.

### Breakup Criteria and Market Structure
- **Dominant platform breakup authority:** Establish clear criteria for when a company has achieved monopolistic or anticompetitive dominance requiring structural separation
- **Vertical integration limits:** Prohibit or unwind vertical integration where one company controls both supply/manufacturing and distribution/retail in ways that exclude competitors
- **Horizontal consolidation limits:** Presumption against mergers in already-concentrated markets; shift burden to merging parties to prove competition will not be harmed
- **Sector-specific rules:** Differentiated approaches for technology platforms, healthcare systems, financial institutions, food/agriculture, telecommunications, and media

### Merger and Acquisition Reform
- **Heightened merger scrutiny:** Strengthen review standards beyond simple price effects to include quality, innovation, labor markets, supplier terms, and political power
- **Acquisition prohibition for dominant platforms:** Ban acquisitions by companies with dominant market positions that would eliminate potential competitors or extend control into adjacent markets (e.g., Facebook acquiring Instagram/WhatsApp, Google acquiring YouTube/competitors, Amazon acquiring e-commerce/logistics competitors)
- **Retrospective merger review:** Authority to unwind past mergers that have proven anticompetitive even if approved at the time

### Platform and Essential Service Regulation
- **Nondiscrimination requirements:** Platforms that serve as essential infrastructure (app stores, e-commerce marketplaces, payment systems, search engines, social networks) cannot discriminate against competitors or favor their own products
- **Interoperability mandates:** Dominant platforms must provide API access, data portability, and technical interoperability to enable competition and prevent lock-in
- **Self-preferencing prohibition:** Platforms cannot use their control of infrastructure to advantage their own products or services in adjacent markets (e.g., Amazon promoting Amazon Basics over third-party sellers, Google promoting Google services in search results)

### Supply Chain Competition
- **Supply chain transparency:** Require transparent pricing and terms in industries where a few distributors control access to goods
- **Anti-exclusivity rules:** Prohibit exclusivity agreements that prevent suppliers from working with multiple distributors or prevent buyers from sourcing from multiple suppliers
- **Distributor separation:** In industries with severe consolidation (food service, pharmaceuticals), consider requiring separation between wholesale distribution and retail/consumer-facing operations
- **Independent business protections:** Establish protections for independent businesses against discriminatory pricing, terms, or access by dominant suppliers or distributors

### Quality, Durability, and Consumer Protection
- **Right to repair:** Require manufacturers to make repair manuals, diagnostic tools, and replacement parts available to consumers and independent repair shops
- **Planned obsolescence prohibition:** Ban design strategies intended to artificially limit product lifespan or force replacement
- **Durability standards:** Establish minimum durability and sustainability requirements for consumer goods to counteract the race-to-the-bottom in product quality that occurs in monopolistic markets
- **Disposability disincentives:** Use extended producer responsibility, deposit systems, or disposal fees to create incentives for durable, repairable, recyclable products

### Enforcement and Institutional Capacity
- **Strengthened antitrust agencies:** Increase funding and authority for Federal Trade Commission and Department of Justice Antitrust Division
- **Private right of action:** Enable consumers, workers, and small businesses to bring antitrust suits with reasonable attorney fee provisions
- **State enforcement support:** Coordination and support for state attorneys general bringing antitrust actions
- **Criminal penalties:** Establish meaningful criminal penalties for anticompetitive conduct including price-fixing, market allocation, bid-rigging, and monopolization
- **Corporate structure limits:** Consider limits on corporate complexity and layering that make antitrust enforcement difficult (e.g., limits on number of subsidiaries, restrictions on shell company structures)

### Market-Specific Reforms

#### Technology Platforms
- Break up or regulate Big Tech companies (Google, Amazon, Facebook/Meta, Apple) that control essential digital infrastructure
- Prohibit use of platform data to compete against platform users
- Require algorithmic transparency and user control over content selection
- Establish competition in app stores, payment systems, and device ecosystems

#### Healthcare
- Limit hospital system consolidation
- Require separation between insurance and provider functions
- Address pharmaceutical market concentration and patent abuse
- Regulate pharmacy benefit managers and restrict vertical integration in drug supply

#### Finance
- Restore Glass-Steagall separation between commercial and investment banking
- Limit bank size relative to GDP
- Break up or regulate "too big to fail" institutions
- Address concentration in payment processing, lending, and investment management

#### Food and Agriculture
- Break up or regulate agribusiness consolidation in seed, chemical, processing, and distribution
- Address meatpacking concentration
- Require fair terms for contract farmers and producers
- Address food service distribution monopolies (Sysco and similar)

#### Media and Telecommunications
- Limit media ownership concentration
- Restore net neutrality and common carrier requirements
- Address local news deserts through competition policy
- Require separation between content and distribution

## Implementation Notes

The antitrust reforms described above require:
1. **Legislative action** to establish new standards, authorities, and enforcement mechanisms
2. **Regulatory rulemaking** by FTC and DOJ to implement standards in specific markets
3. **Judicial interpretation** that moves beyond narrow price-focused consumer welfare standard
4. **State-level coordination** for enforcement and policy that complements federal action
5. **International cooperation** to address global platforms and cross-border corporate structures

The political challenge is that concentrated industries will fight these reforms aggressively. The strategic response is to build coalitions that include:
- Small businesses harmed by dominant competitors
- Workers facing reduced options and bargaining power
- Consumers paying higher prices and receiving lower quality
- Communities affected by consolidation and disinvestment
- Advocates for democracy concerned about corporate political power

Frame antitrust not as technical policy but as fundamental fairness: markets should reward competition and quality, not size and extraction.
