## JUS-POL — Police Use of Force and Weaponry

### JUS-POL-006 — Ban police automatic weapons
**Status:** MISSING  
**Statement:** Ban police automatic weapons

**Notes:** This rule prohibits police departments from possessing or deploying automatic weapons in routine policing operations. Automatic weapons (machine guns, assault rifles capable of sustained automatic fire) are designed for military combat, not law enforcement. Their use escalates violence, undermines de-escalation principles, and militarizes police-community relations.

Specialized tactical units may require access to specific weapons for high-risk situations (active shooter response, hostage rescue, counter-terrorism operations), but these should be strictly limited, subject to oversight, and not part of general patrol equipment. The militarization of routine policing has transformed police departments into occupying forces in many communities, particularly communities of color. Banning automatic weapons for police is part of a broader de-militarization and accountability framework.

This rule does not disarm police — it limits them to weapons appropriate for law enforcement rather than military combat. Standard service pistols, shotguns, and precision rifles for specialized situations remain available. The goal is to prevent the arms-race dynamic where police militarization drives civilian armament and vice versa.

---

### JUS-POL-007 — Ban police weapons of war
**Status:** MISSING  
**Statement:** Ban police weapons of war

**Notes:** This rule extends beyond automatic weapons to prohibit police possession of any weapons designed for military combat rather than law enforcement. This includes:

- Armored personnel carriers and military-grade vehicles (MRAPs, tanks)
- Grenade launchers and explosive devices
- Bayonets and other military combat weapons
- Military-grade body armor and tactical gear designed for combat rather than protection
- Surveillance and weapons systems designed for warfare (armed drones, military-grade surveillance equipment)

The 1033 Program has transferred billions of dollars of surplus military equipment to local police departments, transforming law enforcement into a paramilitary force. This equipment is not designed for community policing, de-escalation, or public safety — it is designed for combat operations against enemy combatants. Its presence in policing escalates violence, undermines trust, and creates a mentality where communities are viewed as battlegrounds rather than neighborhoods to protect.

Exceptions may be necessary for specialized federal units dealing with terrorism or organized crime, but these should be strictly limited, transparent, and subject to oversight. General municipal and county police departments should not possess weapons of war.

This rule addresses the root cause of police violence: when police are equipped like soldiers, they act like soldiers. De-militarization is a prerequisite for accountability, de-escalation, and community trust.

## GUN-REG — General Firearm Regulation Authority

### [PROPOSED] GUN-REG-001 — Constitutional authority to regulate firearms
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Rework the Second Amendment or related constitutional text to explicitly affirm government authority to regulate firearms and weaponry

**Notes:** This establishes clear constitutional authority for federal and state governments to regulate firearms without being blocked by Second Amendment challenges. It does not eliminate gun rights — it clarifies that the right to keep and bear arms coexists with legitimate government regulation. Current constitutional ambiguity creates a legal environment where basic safety measures (background checks, assault weapon bans, training requirements) are challenged as unconstitutional. Explicit constitutional language affirming regulatory authority resolves this uncertainty.

The language should clarify that regulation must be safety-based and proportionate (not arbitrary prohibition) while affirming that weapons of war, unaccountable armed groups, and dangerous individuals can be restricted.

## GUN-BAN — Prohibited Weapons and Ownership

### [PROPOSED] GUN-BAN-001 — Ban private ownership of automatic weapons
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Ban private ownership of automatic weapons

**Notes:** Automatic weapons (machine guns, rifles capable of sustained automatic fire) serve no legitimate civilian purpose and are designed to maximize combat lethality. They have been heavily regulated since 1934 and effectively banned for new civilian manufacture since 1986, but pre-1986 automatic weapons remain legal with federal licensing. This rule prohibits all private civilian ownership, closing this grandfather exception.

---

### [PROPOSED] GUN-BAN-002 — Ban private ownership of weapons of war
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Ban private ownership of weapons of war, including automatic weapons and demilitarized semi-automatic civilian versions designed to evade regulation

**Notes:** This extends beyond automatic weapons to prohibit semi-automatic rifles designed for military combat. AR-15 variants, AK-47 variants, and comparable weapons are designed to maximize casualties through high-capacity magazines, rapid fire, and ergonomic features optimized for sustained shooting. They are not traditional hunting rifles or self-defense handguns. Manufacturers systematically evade regulation by producing "civilian" versions that are functionally equivalent to military weapons.

The definition must be clear enough to prevent evasion: weapons designed for military use, regardless of whether they are technically "automatic," can be prohibited. This does not ban all semi-automatic firearms — many common handguns and hunting rifles are semi-automatic without being designed for mass casualties.

---

### [PROPOSED] GUN-BAN-003 — Ban high-capacity magazines
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Prohibit high-capacity magazines that enable mass-casualty shootings without reloading

**Notes:** High-capacity magazines (typically defined as more than 10 rounds) enable sustained fire without reloading, increasing lethality in mass-shooting scenarios. Limiting magazine capacity does not prevent self-defense or hunting but does reduce casualties in mass-casualty attacks by requiring shooters to reload, creating opportunities for intervention or escape.

## GUN-ACQ — Acquisition and Background Checks

### [PROPOSED] GUN-ACQ-001 — Universal background checks
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Require universal background checks for all firearm acquisitions, including private sales and gun show transactions

**Notes:** Current federal law requires background checks for sales by federally licensed dealers but not for private sales or gun show transactions. This creates a massive loophole where prohibited individuals acquire firearms without background checks. Universal background checks close this loophole, requiring checks for all transfers regardless of seller.

This does not create a gun registry — it simply ensures that every firearm transfer is screened for prohibited purchasers (felons, domestic abusers, individuals under restraining orders, those adjudicated as mentally incompetent). Background checks are only effective if databases are comprehensive and properly maintained.

---

### [PROPOSED] GUN-ACQ-002 — Mandatory waiting periods
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Establish mandatory waiting periods between firearm purchase and delivery

**Notes:** Waiting periods (typically 3–10 days) reduce impulsive gun violence, particularly suicides and crimes of passion. Immediate access to firearms during moments of acute crisis or rage increases lethality. Waiting periods do not prevent gun ownership — they simply introduce a cooling-off period.

Exceptions may be appropriate for individuals who already own firearms (since they have immediate access regardless of waiting periods), though this creates complexity in enforcement.

---

## GUN-TRN — Training and Safety Requirements

### [PROPOSED] GUN-TRN-001 — Mandatory safety training
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Require safety training for firearm ownership and carrying

**Notes:** Gun safety training should cover secure storage, handling, operation, and legal responsibilities. Many gun deaths are accidental, resulting from improper handling or storage. Training reduces preventable accidents and ensures that gun owners understand their legal obligations.

Training requirements must be accessible and not function as a barrier to exercise of rights. Training should be widely available, low-cost or subsidized, and offered in multiple languages.

---

### [PROPOSED] GUN-TRN-002 — Mandatory de-escalation training
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Require de-escalation training for firearm ownership and carrying, particularly for concealed carry

**Notes:** De-escalation training teaches individuals to avoid and defuse confrontations rather than escalating to lethal force. Many defensive gun uses result from avoidable confrontations that escalated unnecessarily. Training in conflict avoidance, verbal de-escalation, and situational awareness reduces preventable shootings.

This is particularly important for concealed-carry permits, where individuals carry firearms in public and may encounter confrontational situations.

---

### [PROPOSED] GUN-TRN-003 — Secure storage requirements
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Require secure storage of firearms to prevent unauthorized access, particularly by children

**Notes:** Unsecured firearms are a leading cause of accidental child deaths and are frequently stolen and used in crimes. Secure storage requirements (locked safes, trigger locks, or comparable measures) prevent unauthorized access. This does not prevent self-defense — quick-access safes allow authorized users to retrieve firearms rapidly while preventing access by children or unauthorized individuals.

Enforcement is challenging but can be addressed through liability for negligent storage (if a child accesses an unsecured firearm and causes harm, the owner faces liability) and public education campaigns.

---

## GUN-MHE — Mental Health Evaluations

### [PROPOSED] GUN-MHE-001 — Narrowly tailored dangerousness-based mental health evaluations
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Require narrowly tailored dangerousness-based mental health evaluations to determine if individuals are likely to harm themselves or others, without categorical exclusion based on diagnosis

**Notes:** Mental health evaluations should assess whether a specific individual presents imminent danger, not whether they have a particular diagnosis. Most people with mental health conditions are not dangerous, and most gun violence is not committed by people with diagnosed mental illness. Categorical exclusions based on diagnosis (e.g., banning gun ownership for anyone with ADHD, autism, depression, or anxiety) are discriminatory and ineffective.

Evaluations must be conducted by trained professionals using evidence-based risk assessment tools. They should focus on specific behaviors and circumstances indicating imminent danger (threats, violent behavior, suicidal ideation, acute crisis) rather than diagnosis alone. Temporary restrictions during acute crises can be appropriate, but permanent categorical bans based on diagnosis are not.

---

### [PROPOSED] GUN-MHE-002 — Prohibition of categorical diagnosis-based exclusions
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Prohibit blanket firearm exclusion based solely on mental health diagnosis such as ADHD, autism, or other conditions that do not inherently indicate dangerousness

**Notes:** This rule prevents discriminatory categorical bans that stigmatize mental health conditions without addressing genuine risk. A diagnosis of ADHD, autism, depression, or anxiety does not make someone dangerous. Risk assessments must be individualized and evidence-based.

## GUN-RFL — Red Flag / Extreme Risk Protection Orders

### [PROPOSED] GUN-RFL-001 — Extreme risk protection orders
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Establish red-flag laws (extreme risk protection orders) allowing family members or law enforcement to petition courts to temporarily remove firearms from individuals presenting immediate danger

**Notes:** Red-flag laws have been effective in preventing suicides and domestic violence homicides. They allow intervention during acute crises without permanent prohibition. The standard must be clear evidence of imminent risk (threats, violent behavior, credible concerns from family or clinicians) with due process protections (judicial review, time limits, right to contest).

Abuse prevention is critical — petitions cannot be used for political targeting or harassment. Clear standards, judicial oversight, and penalties for false petitions provide safeguards.

## GUN-MIL — Militia Regulation and Armed Groups

### [PROPOSED] GUN-MIL-001 — Definition of well regulated militia
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Explicitly define "well regulated militia" and require militias to meet transparency, accountability, and safety standards

**Notes:** The Second Amendment references "well regulated militia" but does not define it. This rule establishes that militias must:
- Maintain public membership records
- Disclose chain of command and officers
- Carry liability insurance
- Submit to regular audits
- Follow state and federally approved training and safety standards
- Coordinate with state and federal oversight authorities

Militias that meet these standards can engage in lawful activities including disaster relief, search and rescue, and community emergency response under appropriate oversight. Those that refuse transparency and accountability cannot claim constitutional protection.

---

### [PROPOSED] GUN-MIL-002 — Prohibition of private armies and mercenary groups
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Explicitly prohibit private armies, mercenary groups, and unaccountable armed formations

**Notes:** Armed groups that refuse transparency, reject democratic accountability, and operate as parallel structures of violence are incompatible with democratic order. This includes:
- Private military companies operating domestically without clear legal authority and oversight
- Armed groups that refuse to disclose membership or leadership
- Mercenary formations that provide armed services for hire without accountability

This does not prohibit security services or regulated militias — it prohibits unaccountable armed formations that represent a threat to democratic governance.

---

### [PROPOSED] GUN-MIL-003 — Militia training standards
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Require state and federal oversight of militia training materials and standards, including mandatory de-escalation and safety training

**Notes:** Militias must follow approved training standards that emphasize safety, de-escalation, coordination with authorities, and lawful use of force. Training for disaster relief, search and rescue, and emergency response should be encouraged and supported. Training for insurrection, violent confrontation with government, or tactical combat against civilians should be prohibited.

---

### [PROPOSED] GUN-MIL-004 — Militia service for disaster relief
**Status:** NEEDS FORMAL INTEGRATION  
**Statement:** Allow and encourage lawful militia service for disaster relief, search and rescue, and community emergency response under clear oversight structures

**Notes:** Regulated militias can provide valuable services during natural disasters, emergencies, and community crises. When coordinated with police, fire departments, National Guard, and emergency management agencies under clear command structures, militias can assist with search and rescue, shelter operations, supply distribution, and community safety.

This provides a constructive outlet for militia activity while maintaining accountability and preventing parallel structures of violence.
