# Equal Justice — Policy Pillars

The following policy domains are built on this foundation:

## Equal Justice and Policing
Governs how state force, punishment, detention, and legal accountability operate to protect public safety while applying the law fairly, consistently, and equally to all people. Covers policing reform, qualified immunity, excessive force, prosecutorial accountability, sentencing reform, and the design of a criminal justice system built for rehabilitation and safety rather than extraction and mass incarceration.
→ [overview.md](./equal_justice_and_policing/overview.md) | [policy rules](./equal_justice_and_policing/policy.md)

## Immigration
Establishes a humane, orderly, and rights-respecting immigration system that protects due process, family integrity, and equal dignity. Covers lawful pathways to status and citizenship, asylum standards, detention limits, enforcement accountability, and the principle that every person subject to the authority of the state is entitled to basic procedural protections regardless of immigration status.
→ [overview.md](./immigration/overview.md) | [policy rules](./immigration/policy.md)

## Rights and Civil Liberties
Makes rights explicit, modern, durable, and harder to roll back. Covers bodily autonomy, equal protection, privacy, freedom of conscience, anti-discrimination, and the structural protections required to keep rights from becoming theoretical. This pillar is shared with the Real Freedom foundation; its placement here reflects the equal-protection and anti-discrimination dimensions of rights enforcement.
→ [overview.md](./rights_and_civil_liberties/overview.md) | [policy rules](./rights_and_civil_liberties/policy.md)

## Foreign Policy
Establishes a foreign policy grounded in human rights, multilateral cooperation, and democratic accountability rather than arms sales, unilateral intervention, and covert action unchecked by Congress. Covers human rights conditionality, diplomatic primacy, war powers enforcement, international institution engagement, and the obligation to repair damage done by prior foreign policy failures.
