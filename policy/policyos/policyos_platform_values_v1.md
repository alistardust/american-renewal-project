# PolicyOS Platform Values v1

Generated: 2026-04-26

## Purpose

This document defines the platform values that sit above PolicyOS.

These values are not domain policies. They are the moral and political
commitments that PolicyOS is meant to protect, operationalize, and make
durable across the platform.

This document uses an explicit `floor + duty` model.

That means each value does two jobs at once:

- `Floor`: a negative prohibition — what government and policy must not do.
  The floor protects against erosion, violation, and deprivation of the right.
  In practice: "The government shall not infringe, restrict, or deny..."

- `Duty`: a positive obligation — what government and policy must actively do.
  The duty requires guaranteeing, securing, and building toward the right.
  In practice: "The government shall ensure, guarantee, or actively secure..."

Together they allow specificity without prescribing a single implementation.
The floor sets the hard limit. The duty sets the affirmative requirement.
How the duty is met is a question of policy design, not values.

These are not a sliding scale. The floor is not a "minimum acceptable step"
and the duty is not an "aspiration." Both are requirements. The floor is what
policy may never fall beneath. The duty is what policy must affirmatively build.

**On the duty side and fiscal authority:** The duty side of each value requires
affirmative provision — material things that rules alone cannot deliver without
resources, institutional capacity, and fiscal authority. This document declares
the duty as an absolute requirement, but recognizes that fulfilling it requires
more than rule design. Policy proposed under a duty obligation must therefore
specify the funding mechanism, authorization path, and institutional structure
required to actually deliver the provision. A duty that is declared but not
resourced is hollow.

PolicyOS should therefore be read not only as a system for preventing bad
policy, but as a system for requiring policy to affirmatively realize the
platform’s core commitments.

They should function as the anchor for:

- the PolicyOS principles layer
- the PolicyOS authoring OS, especially the `NORM` family
- scope decisions about what belongs in canon, overlays, pillars, or prose

## One-sentence definition

The Freedom and Dignity Project is grounded in human dignity, equal standing,
real liberty, democratic self-government, accountable power, transparency,
material security, ecological habitability, truthfulness in public systems,
enforceable fairness, and durable protection against capture, domination, and
neglect.

## The values

### 1. Human dignity

Every person must be treated as fully human, never as disposable, degradingly
managed, or reduced to an obstacle, commodity, or data point.

Policy-writing floor:
Rules may not rely on humiliation, cruelty, abandonment, or deprivation as
tools of governance.

Policy-writing duty:
Rules must actively preserve humane treatment, non-degrading access, practical
respect, and recognition of every person as fully entitled to rights, care, and
public concern.

### 2. Equal standing

No one should be consigned to second-class treatment before the law, public
institutions, or essential systems because of race, origin, wealth, geography,
status, disability, or administrative burden.

Policy-writing floor:
Rules must not create unequal classes, selective burdens, arbitrary exclusions,
or practical denial of equal access.

Policy-writing duty:
Rules must actively secure equal protection, equal usability, and meaningful
access to remedy across status, geography, and institutional setting.

### 3. Real liberty

Freedom means meaningful agency, privacy, bodily autonomy, expressive freedom,
and protection against coercion by both public and private power.

Policy-writing floor:
Rules must not impose coercion, domination, surveillance, or constraint in ways
that hollow out real freedom while preserving only formal rights language.

Policy-writing duty:
Rules must actively protect meaningful agency, privacy, bodily autonomy,
expressive freedom, and the practical ability to make real choices.

### 4. Democratic self-government

People must be able to govern the institutions that govern them.

Policy-writing floor:
Rules must not entrench corruption, manipulation, exclusion, or institutional
capture that blocks public self-government.

Policy-writing duty:
Rules must actively strengthen fair representation, public control, civic
legibility, and resistance to corruption, capture, and manipulation. Rules must
also preserve and expand the practical capacity for civic participation: the
ability of ordinary people to understand, engage with, organize around, and be
heard by the systems and decisions that govern them — not merely as a formal
right but as a practical reality.

### 5. Accountable power

No public or private actor may exercise consequential power without oversight,
review, enforceable limits, and meaningful consequences for abuse.

Policy-writing floor:
No rule should create unreviewable authority, immunity in practice, or systems
that place actors above the law.

Policy-writing duty:
Rules must actively create review paths, oversight mechanisms, attributable
decision-making, and meaningful consequences for abuse or failure.

### 6. Transparency

People must be able to see how consequential public and delegated systems work,
who is acting, what standards are being used, and what records exist.

Policy-writing floor:
Rules must not rely on hidden governance, inaccessible procedure, secret
standards, or opaque decision paths.

Policy-writing duty:
Rules must actively require visible standards, visible records, visible
decision paths, and visible responsible actors at the level needed for review
and democratic control.

### 7. Material security

People need stable access to the conditions of a dignified life in order to be
meaningfully free.

Policy-writing floor:
Rules must not treat deprivation, precarity, or denial of basic needs as
acceptable background conditions of freedom.

Policy-writing duty:
Rules must actively secure stable practical access to healthcare, housing, food,
education, income stability, and essential public goods.

### 8. Ecological habitability

A free society has a duty to protect the shared ecological conditions that make
freedom, health, and human dignity possible, including clean air, clean water,
habitable land, safe food systems, and a livable climate.

Policy-writing floor:
Rules must not treat environmental degradation, pollution, or ecological
extraction as acceptable tradeoffs where they destroy the conditions of life or
offload harm onto disempowered communities.

Policy-writing duty:
Rules must actively protect, restore, and steward the shared ecological
conditions of freedom and health, including clean air, clean water, safe food
systems, habitable land, and a livable climate.

### 9. Truthfulness and legibility

People must be able to understand the systems that govern them and the claims
made in their name.

Policy-writing floor:
Rules must not depend on deception, inaccessible reasoning, hidden assumptions,
or ambiguity that prevents ordinary people from understanding what the system is
doing.

Policy-writing duty:
Rules must be plain-language, explainable, traceable, and grounded in auditable
public reasoning.

### 10. Enforceable fairness

Fairness is not symbolic. Similar cases must be treated similarly, burdens and
benefits must be reviewable, and violations must have remedies with teeth.

Policy-writing floor:
Rules must not permit arbitrary treatment, unreviewable asymmetry, or purely
symbolic remedies that leave unfairness intact in practice.

Policy-writing duty:
Rules must actively specify standards, review paths, detection mechanisms, and
consequences strong enough to make fairness real.

### 11. Durability against capture and neglect

Policy should be designed to withstand bad actors, institutional drift,
technological change, capture, loophole exploitation, and deliberate
under-enforcement.

Policy-writing floor:
Rules must not be written in ways that invite easy evasion, predictable
capture, silent decay, or bad-faith reinterpretation of their baseline purpose.

Policy-writing duty:
Rules must actively include maintenance logic, anti-evasion design, bounded
interpretive flexibility, and mechanisms for repair when systems fail.

## How these values constrain PolicyOS

These values are meant to answer questions like:

- what kinds of rules are disallowed even if they are technically tidy?
- what kinds of ambiguity are unacceptable?
- what kinds of tradeoffs require explicit justification?
- what forms of enforcement weakness count as moral failure, not just design weakness?
- what harms must be treated as cross-platform concerns rather than isolated pillar issues?

They also establish the operating logic for normative review:

- a rule fails if it violates the floor of a value
- a rule is incomplete if it avoids violation but does not satisfy the duty of the relevant value
- a rule is strongest when it both protects the floor and fulfills the duty

In practice, this means:

- the PolicyOS principles layer should be derived from these values
- the PolicyOS authoring layer should force every major rule to identify and
  satisfy the relevant values
- pillars should be reviewed not only for completeness, but for fidelity to
  this values layer

The five foundations remain the top-level political architecture of the

These values are the more precise bedrock underneath them.

- `Accountable Power` draws heavily on accountable power, transparency, and durability against abuse.
- `Clean Democracy` draws heavily on democratic self-government, accountable power, enforceable fairness, and anti-capture durability.
- `Equal Justice` draws heavily on dignity, equal standing, enforceable fairness, and truthfulness in public systems.
- `Real Freedom` draws heavily on real liberty, transparency, accountable power, and protection against coercion by concentrated power.
- `Freedom to Thrive` draws heavily on material security, ecological habitability, dignity, and equal practical access.

## Commentary: value tensions

These values work together most of the time. But they are not identical, and
some produce genuine tension when applied to specific policy questions. This is
not a flaw — tension between real values is a sign of a serious framework.
PAOS-NORM-0008 requires that these tensions be surfaced explicitly and resolved
in the drafting, rather than hidden. This section maps the major recurring
tensions and provides resolution principles so that different pillars resolve
them consistently.

**Privacy vs. Transparency (Value 3 vs. Value 6)**

Transparency requires that consequential public systems be visible, auditable,
and legible to democratic oversight. Privacy requires that individuals be
protected from surveillance, coercion, and exposure of personal information.

Resolution principle: The default is privacy for individuals and transparency
for institutions. An individual has a privacy interest in their own data,
conduct, and personal decisions. An institution or public actor has no privacy
interest in the exercise of public authority. Where the tension is between the
state demanding individual transparency and the individual's right to privacy,
the individual's privacy right prevails unless overridden by a specific,
articulable public-interest justification narrow enough to pass scrutiny under
Value 3.

**Individual liberty vs. Material security (Value 3 vs. Value 7)**

The duty of material security requires affirmative provision — healthcare,
housing, food, education, income stability. Provision can shade into
conditionality: "you receive this if you comply with that." Conditionality on
behavior can itself be coercive.

Resolution principle: Provision of material security must be designed to expand
real choices, not compel specific behaviors. Conditionality is permissible only
where the condition itself is non-coercive, passes scrutiny under Value 3, and
is genuinely necessary to the provision's purpose — not a device for
social control.

**Ecological protection vs. Material security (Value 8 vs. Value 7)**

Environmental policy sometimes requires restricting extractive industry or
economic activity. Those restrictions can reduce employment and income in
affected communities, working against material security for real people in the
near term.

Resolution principle: Environmental and labor interests are not opposed by
default — ecological degradation itself undermines long-term material security.
Where genuine short-term material hardship results from ecological protection,
policy must include active transition mechanisms (Value 7 duty) rather than
treating the tradeoff as acceptable collateral damage. Neither value may be
used to permanently override the other.

**Democratic majority vs. Minority equal standing (Value 4 vs. Value 2)**

Democratic self-government can produce majority decisions that impose selective
burdens on minority groups. Legitimate democratic process does not make the
result fair.

Resolution principle: Equal standing is not subject to majority override.
Value 2's floor — no unequal classes, no selective burdens, no practical denial
of equal access — applies regardless of the democratic legitimacy of the process
that produced the outcome. Democratic legitimacy is necessary for policy; it is
not sufficient.

**Durability vs. Democratic self-government (Value 11 vs. Value 4)**

Rules designed to resist capture, evasion, and drift are harder to change.
Hard-to-change rules can entrench the preferences of a prior democratic
majority over a current one.

Resolution principle: Durability protects the floor, not the policy above it.
Floors must be durable; the policies that fulfill the duty above the floor
should be democratically revisable. Durability requirements should never be
used to insulate policy from legitimate democratic revision — only to protect
against bad-faith erosion, capture, or administrative neglect.

## Amendment protocol

This document is locked as the canonical PolicyOS values layer. "Locked" does
not mean permanent. It means: no casual revision, no incremental drift, no
amendments made without deliberate process.

The following protocol governs amendment:

- **Grounds for amendment:** A value may be amended where its content is
  found to be materially incomplete (a significant dimension of the value is
  unaddressed), internally inconsistent, or in irreconcilable conflict with
  another value. Clarification of interpretation is not amendment.
- **Proposal:** Any proposed amendment must be written as a specific tracked
  change with a stated rationale, the tension or gap it resolves, and the
  pillar or rule context in which the problem was identified.
- **Review:** A proposed amendment must be reviewed against all eleven values,
  the definitions document, and any existing pillar rules that rely on the
  current formulation.
- **Traceability:** Amendments must be versioned and traceable. Prior versions
  are retained. The amendment record must show what changed, when, and why.
- **Pillar update obligation:** Any amendment that changes the meaning of a
  floor or duty must trigger a review of all existing pillar rules that
  reference the affected value, with identified rules updated or formally
  grandfathered.

Until a formal governance body is established, the platform maintainer
(currently the project originator) is the amendment authority. This is a
placeholder pending establishment of the PolicyOS governance model.
