# Teacher Pay, Rights, and Shortage: A Policy Research Brief

**Freedom & Dignity Project — Policy Research Series**
*Prepared: July 2025*
*Topic area: Education / Labor Rights*

---

## Executive Summary

- **The teacher wage penalty reached a record 26.6% in 2023**, meaning teachers earn only 73.4 cents for every dollar earned by comparably educated workers in other professions — down from 93.9 cents in 1996. Even when accounting for benefits, the total compensation gap remains at approximately 16.7% [1][2].
- **National average teacher salary was $72,030 in 2023–24**, with a wide state spread ranging from California ($95,160) to West Virginia ($52,870). Average starting salaries of $46,526 trail the entry wages of most comparable professions [3].
- **More than 400,000 teaching positions** (approximately 1 in 8) were either unfilled or staffed by teachers lacking full certification for their assignments in 2023–24, with high-poverty and high-minority schools facing the steepest shortages [4].
- **Root causes are systemic:** chronic low pay, unsustainable workloads (teachers average 53 work hours per week vs. 44 for similar workers), burnout rates of 53–60%, erosion of collective bargaining rights, and a deteriorating political climate in restriction-heavy states [5][6].
- **The teacher pipeline is still well below historic norms**, despite modest stabilization since 2019. Completion rates from teacher preparation programs dropped from 41.2% of enrollees in 2014–15 to 26.9% in 2020–21 [7].
- **Collective bargaining correlates with 24–26% higher average teacher pay**. Anti-union legislation (e.g., Wisconsin's Act 10) increased turnover, reduced wages, and suppressed teacher voice without measurable short-term improvement in student outcomes [8][9].
- **Classroom censorship laws are fueling the crisis:** Book bans tripled to over 10,000 instances in 2023–24. Surveys document a "culture of fear" among Florida teachers directly attributing curriculum restrictions to attrition [10][11].
- **The workforce is not representative of students served:** 52% of K–12 students are students of color, but only 21–22% of teachers are. Research links same-race teacher assignment to better academic outcomes and lower suspension rates for students of color [12].
- **Evidence-based solutions exist** — including substantial pay raises, teacher residency programs, grow-your-own pipelines, restored collective bargaining, housing assistance, and class size reduction — but require sustained political will and public investment.

## Part 1: Teacher Pay

### 1.1 National Average Salaries

According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics' 2023 Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics survey, elementary school teachers (excluding special education) had a mean annual wage of **$70,740** and a median of **$63,680**, with the 90th percentile earning above $104,440 [13]. High school teachers had a median annual wage of approximately **$64,700** in 2023–24 [14].

The National Education Association's 2023–24 Teacher Salary Benchmark Report, which draws on district-level salary schedule data, found a national average public school teacher salary of **$72,030** — a 3.8% nominal increase from the prior year, representing the largest one-year jump in recent memory but still insufficient to keep pace with broader wage gains [3]. The NEA reports:

- **Average starting salary:** $46,526 (a 4.4% increase from 2022–23 — the largest 1-year jump in 15 years)
- **Average maximum salary:** $84,272
- Private school teachers earn a median of approximately **$57,600**, compared to $64,000 for public school teachers [14]

More than 3,600 school districts now have starting salaries at or above $50,000 — up from a much smaller share in prior years — and over 800 districts offer new teachers at least $60,000 [3].

### 1.3 The Teacher Wage Penalty

The Economic Policy Institute publishes annual analyses of the teacher wage penalty — a regression-adjusted estimate of the earnings gap between public school teachers and other full-time college-educated workers with comparable education and experience. Key findings from EPI's 2023 and 2024 analyses [1][2][15]:

- **2023 wage penalty: 26.6%** — teachers earned only 73.4 cents for every dollar earned by similarly educated non-teachers. This is the largest gap on record since EPI began tracking.
- **Historical comparison:** In 1996, teachers earned 93.9 cents on the dollar relative to comparable workers; the gap has grown continuously across decades, with the steepest deterioration occurring from 2009 through 2023.
- **By gender:** The penalty is larger for men (36.3%) than women (21.4%) in absolute terms, reflecting that men have greater access to higher-paying alternatives outside education. However, within teaching, men still earn more than women (see Section 5.2).
- **Total compensation penalty:** Even accounting for benefits (pensions, health insurance) that teachers typically receive at higher rates, the total compensation gap was **16.7% in 2023**, meaning benefits partially but do not fully offset the wage disadvantage.
- **Benefits gap is narrowing:** As public pension obligations are restructured and healthcare costs shift to employees, the benefits premium that once partially compensated for lower wages has eroded [1].

The EPI analysis controls for education level, experience, hours worked, and regional cost of living. The penalty cannot be explained by teachers working less — RAND survey data find teachers work an average of **nine more hours per week** than comparable working adults (53 vs. 44 hours) [5].

### 1.4 Real Wage Trends Over Time (Inflation-Adjusted)

Inflation-adjusted weekly wages for public school teachers declined significantly from 2009 to 2022 and have only partially recovered [1][15]:

- Over the decade leading to 2022, **real teacher wages fell approximately $46**, while real wages for other college graduates rose approximately $220 [15].
- Between 2021 and 2022, inflation-adjusted weekly teacher wages dropped from $1,457 to $1,329 — a nearly 9% real decline in a single year driven by post-pandemic inflation [15].
- 2023 saw a modest 1.7% increase in nominal weekly wages, but the gain did not materially close the penalty gap [1].
- Inflation-adjusted real wages grew in only a handful of states over the past decade, notably New Mexico, Utah, Idaho, Colorado, and California — each of which made deliberate, legislatively-funded investments in teacher compensation [3].
- The NEA's 2023–24 report notes that even with nominal increases, when adjusted for inflation, teachers nationally earn approximately **5% less than they did a decade ago** [3].

### 1.5 Starting vs. Experienced Salary Trajectories

Teacher salary schedules in most states are structured around "step-and-lane" systems, where pay increases annually for experience ("steps") and with additional education credentials ("lanes"). Key patterns [3][5][16]:

- **Starting salaries are the most visible recruitment signal.** The $46,526 national average starting salary is well below entry wages in competing fields requiring comparable education.
- **Salary compression is a structural problem.** The ratio of average maximum to average starting salary is roughly 1.8:1 nationally — meaning a 30-year career teacher earns less than twice what a brand-new teacher earns, compared to steeper trajectories in most professional fields.
- **Experienced teacher shortages are distinct from entry-level shortages.** Because experienced teachers receive tenure protections but still earn relatively modest salaries, some leave mid-career for administration or private sector roles; others stay but feel financially constrained [5].
- RAND's 2024 survey found that **only about one-third of teachers say their pay is adequate**, compared to roughly half of other comparably educated workers; teachers estimated that a raise of approximately $16,000–17,000 would be needed to feel adequately compensated [5].

### 1.6 Pay by School Type

**Public schools** account for approximately 90% of K–12 enrollment and set salary by district-level bargaining or legislative mandate. Their median is approximately $64,000 [14].

**Charter schools**, while publicly funded, operate under varying rules. Compensation structures are more variable — some match district pay scales, others pay below, and some urban "no-excuses" charter chains pay above average to attract high performers. Charter school teachers are less likely to be unionized and, on average, earn somewhat less than their district counterparts [VERIFY: systematic charter-vs-district salary comparison, most recent year].

**Private schools** pay a median of approximately $57,600 — roughly $6,400 less than the public school median — but offer a wider variance. Elite private schools may pay well above public salaries; religious schools often pay substantially less [14].

### 1.7 Geographic Disparities: Rural, Urban, Suburban

- **Urban** school districts, particularly in high-cost cities (New York City, San Francisco, Boston, Seattle), pay substantially above national averages, but cost-of-living offsets real purchasing power. A $75,000 salary in San Francisco may provide less housing security than a $55,000 salary in rural Iowa.
- **Suburban** districts tend to have the highest nominal salaries in many states, with well-funded property tax bases supporting better compensation.
- **Rural** districts face a compound disadvantage: lower nominal salaries, fewer amenities, housing market constraints (often too illiquid for young professionals), and limited spousal employment options. Rural teachers on average earn less than urban or suburban peers, even controlling for state effects [VERIFY: specific rural-urban salary gap estimate, most recent national data].
- Housing costs have become a front-line recruitment and retention crisis in high-cost metros. In California, more than **one-third of public school employees are "rent-burdened,"** spending over 30% of their income on housing — making teacher salaries functionally inadequate even when nominally above national averages [17].

## Part 2: Teacher Rights

### 2.1 Collective Bargaining Rights by State

Public school teacher collective bargaining rights vary dramatically by state, falling into three broad categories:

**States with statutory collective bargaining rights** (typically with a Public Employment Relations Board or similar enforcement mechanism): California, Illinois, New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Washington, Connecticut, Hawaii, Iowa, Maryland, Michigan, Minnesota, and most other Northern and Western states. These states collectively tend to have higher average teacher salaries and lower vacancy rates [9][18].

**States with no law or restricted rights** — where collective bargaining is either expressly prohibited, not authorized, or left entirely to local board discretion without state backing: Texas, Georgia, North Carolina, South Carolina, Arkansas, and to varying degrees Alabama, Mississippi, Missouri, and Tennessee [9][18].

- **Texas** explicitly prohibits collective bargaining for public school teachers. Teachers may form associations but cannot legally compel a district to negotiate [18].
- **Georgia and North Carolina** prohibit collective bargaining for all public employees by statute [18].
- **Virginia** historically prohibited it statewide, though some localities have recently been authorized to allow it on a discretionary basis [18].
- **Indiana** allows bargaining but restricts the scope to wages and benefits only, excluding most working conditions and class size [18].

**States with intermediate frameworks** (e.g., "meet and confer" requirements where districts must consult but not agree): a smaller cluster, largely in the South and Mountain West [18][19].

Only **11 states** explicitly permit teacher strikes; most permit bargaining but prohibit work stoppages [19]. States with collective bargaining rights pay teachers approximately **24–26% more** on average than states without such rights, a difference that persists even after controlling for cost of living and state fiscal capacity [3][9].

### 2.2 Tenure Protections: How They Work, and the Debate

Teacher tenure in the United States refers to a contractual right to continued employment that can only be revoked through formal cause, typically following a probationary period.

**How tenure works:** Most states require teachers to work a probationary period of two to five years before they may earn "professional employment status" or tenure. Once conferred, tenure requires that dismissal or non-renewal proceed through a formal due process procedure — typically requiring a statement of cause, an opportunity to respond, and in many states an administrative hearing or arbitration [20].

**Arguments for tenure:**
- Protects academic freedom: teachers who fear arbitrary dismissal may self-censor or avoid challenging content, suppressing the intellectual environment.
- Guards against discriminatory or politically motivated dismissal.
- Makes teaching competitive with private-sector careers that offer implicit long-term employment security.
- Evidence suggests tenure increases job satisfaction and reduces intention to leave [20].

**Arguments against tenure:**
- Critics argue that removing consistently ineffective teachers is burdensome and costly; a widely cited New York study found dismissal procedures could take years and cost tens of thousands of dollars.
- Tenure may create inequitable assignment patterns: when schools must lay off teachers, seniority rules may force cuts of effective younger teachers over ineffective senior ones.

**What the research shows:** No strong peer-reviewed evidence demonstrates that tenure itself produces better or worse student outcomes on average. The outcomes depend heavily on how effectively districts evaluate and support teachers during the probationary period, and whether post-tenure evaluation systems are robust enough to identify persistent underperformance [20].

### 2.3 Due Process in Discipline and Termination

Due process protections for teachers arise from two sources: constitutional protections (for teachers with property interest in their positions under *Board of Regents v. Roth*, 408 U.S. 564, 1972) and statutory or contractual protections in state tenure laws and collective bargaining agreements [20].

Key procedural elements typically include:
- Written notice of allegations
- Opportunity to review evidence
- Right to a hearing before an impartial decision-maker
- Right to representation (union representation where applicable)
- Right to appeal

Where collective bargaining agreements exist, they typically strengthen these protections by adding arbitration rights. In non-bargaining states, teachers generally depend on statutory notice-and-hearing rights, which are more limited and more subject to change through legislative action [9][20].

### 2.4 Anti-Union Legislation and Its Effects

**Wisconsin Act 10 (2011)** is the most studied modern case of public-sector collective bargaining restriction. The law eliminated most collective bargaining rights for public school teachers and other public workers (police and firefighters were exempted). Research findings include [8][21]:

- **Salary erosion:** Average teacher salaries in Wisconsin fell relative to comparable states post-Act 10. Districts gained unilateral authority over pay, leading to stagnation or reduction, particularly for experienced teachers.
- **Benefits cuts:** Health and pension contribution structures were renegotiated unilaterally by school boards, shifting costs to teachers.
- **Increased turnover:** Multiple studies documented increased teacher turnover after Act 10, with experienced teachers disproportionately more likely to retire or exit.
- **Union membership collapse:** Wisconsin public sector union membership dropped dramatically after Act 10.
- **Short-term student outcomes:** Most studies found no immediate negative impact on standardized test scores in the first few years following enactment.
- **Long-term concerns:** Researchers note that eroding the teacher experience base and reducing the profession's attractiveness may have cumulative, lagged effects on teacher quality and student achievement that standardized test data have yet to fully capture.

**Right-to-work laws** in approximately 27 states prohibit union security clauses requiring all employees in a bargaining unit to pay union dues or fees. Applied to education, right-to-work laws weaken teacher unions' financial capacity and bargaining leverage. Research on their effects finds lower public-sector wages and benefits, particularly for women and workers of color who benefit most from union pay standardization [21].

*Janus v. AFSCME* (2018) (U.S. Supreme Court) further weakened public-sector unions by prohibiting "agency fees" from non-members nationwide, effectively extending right-to-work principles to all public employees including teachers in all 50 states. The impact on teacher union membership, bargaining power, and compensation trajectories is an ongoing area of research [VERIFY: post-Janus teacher union membership trend data, 2019–2024].

### 2.5 Academic Freedom and Classroom Censorship Laws

The current period represents an unprecedented wave of state legislative intervention into K–12 classroom content. Documenting the scope:

**Book bans:** PEN America reported that book bans in U.S. schools **nearly tripled** to over 10,000 instances in the 2023–24 school year, up from approximately 3,362 the prior year [10]. The vast majority of bans were concentrated in Florida and Iowa, where state laws mandating removal of challenged books during review resulted in mass removals across entire districts. Utah and South Carolina also enacted laws facilitating statewide bans [10][22].

The books most commonly banned feature LGBTQ+ characters or themes, address race and racism, or depict sexual experience — including content related to sexual abuse and assault [10].

**"Educational gag orders":** Since 2021, **47 distinct educational gag order laws** have been enacted across U.S. states, with more than 110 additional bills introduced in 2023 alone [11][22]. These laws restrict or prohibit classroom discussion of:
- Structural racism, systemic inequality, and "divisive concepts" about American history
- Gender identity and sexual orientation (the broad category of "Don't Say Gay"-type laws)
- DEI frameworks and equity-oriented curriculum

Florida's HB 1557 (the "Parental Rights in Education" law, 2022) prohibits classroom instruction on sexual orientation or gender identity in kindergarten through grade 3, with vaguer restrictions for older grades. It has been used as a model for similar legislation in other states [11].

**Effects on teachers:** Surveys of Florida educators conducted by researchers from the University of California, San Diego and NYU Steinhardt found that nearly all respondents had direct experience with restriction-related censorship pressure. Teachers reported being afraid to discuss the Civil Rights Movement, Jim Crow laws, or LGBTQ history, citing fear of disciplinary action or termination. Researchers characterized this as a "culture of fear" that is directly accelerating teacher attrition in the state [23].

**Higher education:** Attacks on academic freedom increasingly target tenure, DEI offices, and faculty governance structures in public universities — a shift from earlier laws focused primarily on K–12 content [11].

### 2.6 Protections for Teachers Who Speak Out

Federal and state whistleblower protections for public school teachers are inconsistent and often insufficient.

- **Federal law:** Teachers employed by public school districts are public employees and thus entitled to some First Amendment speech protections under *Garcetti v. Ceballos* (2006), but this case significantly narrowed those protections. Speech made pursuant to official duties (e.g., a teacher's classroom instruction) receives no First Amendment protection, meaning districts may discipline or terminate teachers for classroom conduct that supervisors disapprove of.
- **State whistleblower laws:** Many states have general public employee whistleblower statutes, but their scope and enforcement mechanisms vary widely. Teachers in non-bargaining states have fewer procedural protections when they face retaliation for reporting safety violations, voicing professional concerns, or engaging in advocacy.
- **Union representation:** In states with collective bargaining, union representation provides a practical shield against arbitrary retaliation through grievance procedures and arbitration. In non-bargaining states, teachers lack this protection [9][18].
- **Curriculum restriction laws** create an additional suppression dynamic: teachers may self-censor not because they have been disciplined, but because they reasonably fear they might be. This chilling effect is harder to document and impossible to litigate [23].

## Part 3: Teacher Shortages

### 3.1 Scale and Scope of the Shortage

By any measure, the United States faces a structural and worsening teacher shortage:

- **NCES data, 2023–24:** 86% of public schools reported difficulties hiring teachers for the 2023–24 school year [4].
- **Understaffing rates:** 45% of schools reported being understaffed in 2023–24, down from 53% in 2022–23 but still representing a widespread crisis. High-poverty schools reported understaffing at substantially higher rates [4].
- **Vacancy and under-certification:** The Learning Policy Institute's 2024 update, drawing on state-reported data from 30 states and DC, estimated **41,920 unfilled teacher positions** and approximately **365,044 teachers lacking full certification** for their assigned subjects across 49 states and DC. Combined, this means roughly **400,000 positions — approximately 1 in 8 — were either vacant or filled by teachers not fully qualified** for their assignments [4][24].
- **Fall 2022 snapshot:** 44% of public schools reported at least one vacant teaching position in fall 2022. Among high-poverty schools, the vacancy rate was **57%**; in high-minority schools, it was **60%** [25].

These figures undercount the true shortage because they do not capture schools that simply avoided reporting vacancies by hiring underqualified teachers, requiring existing staff to cover multiple subjects, or eliminating courses.

### 3.2 Subjects and Regions Most Affected

Shortages are not evenly distributed. The most persistent and acute shortages by subject area, as consistently reported across state designation data and NCES surveys [4][24][25]:

- **Special education** — the most universally cited shortage area, present in virtually every state for years, driven by higher emotional demands, greater administrative burden, and inadequate compensation differentials
- **Physical and life sciences (STEM broadly)** — teachers with science or mathematics credentials have strong private-sector alternatives
- **Mathematics** — the large wage penalty is particularly acute for math-credentialed individuals
- **Foreign languages**
- **Career and Technical Education (CTE)**
- **English Language Learner (ELL) / bilingual education**

Geographically, the worst-affected areas are:
- **High-poverty urban districts**, where high need, challenging working conditions, and historical underinvestment compound
- **Rural districts**, which face compounding disadvantages: lower salaries, geographic isolation, housing illiquidity, limited spousal employment opportunities, and limited access to professional development [VERIFY: specific vacancy rate comparison rural vs. national average, most recent year]

### 3.3 Root Causes

Decades of research and practitioner surveys converge on a consistent set of root causes. These are not competing explanations — they reinforce each other.

**a. Low compensation relative to alternatives.** RAND's 2024 State of the American Teacher survey found that on average, teacher base pay is approximately **$18,000 less than comparable professions**. Only about one-third of teachers described their pay as adequate, compared to roughly half of other comparable workers [5].

**b. Excessive workload.** Teachers report working an average of **53 hours per week**, compared to 44 for other comparably educated workers. A substantial portion of this work is uncompensated — evenings and weekends spent grading, planning, communicating with families, and performing administrative tasks [5][6].

**c. Burnout.** RAND surveys document that **53–60% of teachers report burnout** — approximately twice the rate of comparable working adults. Burnout rates have not returned to pre-pandemic levels and remain a primary driver of attrition intentions [5][6].

**d. Student behavior and support deficits.** Post-pandemic, teacher surveys consistently document worsening classroom behavior and mental health challenges among students. Teachers report feeling underprepported by administration and inadequately trained or resourced to address student mental health needs [5][6].

**e. Administrative burden and professional autonomy.** Teachers report losing time to standardized testing, mandated reporting, and procedural compliance. Many cite the erosion of professional judgment and autonomy — increased micromanagement of what they can teach — as a significant source of dissatisfaction [5][11][23].

**f. Political attacks on educators.** Curriculum restriction laws, book ban campaigns, and hostile legislative rhetoric have materially affected teacher morale and professional identity. Florida survey data document a direct causal link between curriculum restriction laws and teacher attrition [23].

**g. Housing costs in high-cost-of-living areas.** In coastal metros and other high-cost areas, teacher salaries are insufficient to afford housing near the schools where they work. One-third of California school employees are rent-burdened [17]. Housing insecurity is an underappreciated but growing driver of shortages in high-cost regions.

**h. Inequities within the workforce.** Black teachers, female teachers, and teachers in high-need schools bear disproportionate shares of these burdens while typically receiving lower compensation (see Part 5).

### 3.4 The Teacher Pipeline: Enrollment, Completion, and Attrition

**Long-term enrollment decline:** Teacher preparation program enrollment dropped by approximately **45% from 2010 to 2018–19** — representing roughly 300,000 fewer candidates entering the pipeline annually. This decline was driven by falling career attractiveness relative to alternatives, declining public faith in education as an institution, and demographic shifts in college enrollment [7][26].

**Partial stabilization (2018–2022):** Title II data analyzed by researchers at Penn State found an approximately **12% increase in teacher preparation enrollment** between 2018–19 and 2021–22, with strong rebounds in Arkansas, Ohio, Louisiana, and Maryland. Eleven states continued to decline [26][27].

**Current enrollment level (2023–24):** The Title II report documents approximately **590,158 total enrollees** across 2,219 provider organizations — roughly stable from 2021–22, and still well below peak enrollment from the early 2010s [27].

**Completion rates** are a more concerning indicator. Completion rates dropped from **41.2% of enrollees in 2014–15** to approximately **26.9% in 2020–21**, meaning fewer than three in ten people who begin teacher preparation programs actually complete them and enter the profession [7].

**COVID-era impact:** The pandemic accelerated existing trends. Teacher turnover, which ran at approximately 6% pre-pandemic, rose during COVID and remained elevated at around **7% in 2023–24**. While declining from peak pandemic levels, it has not returned to baseline [5][28].

**Who is leaving — and why:** Among those who exit, mid-career teachers (5–15 years experience) represent a particularly costly loss; they leave at the peak of their effectiveness before the profession has fully recouped its investment in their development. The leading reasons cited are: job stress and dissatisfaction, inadequate pay, and excessive workload — in roughly that order [5][6].

### 3.5 Effect of Political Climate on Recruitment and Retention

The politicization of K–12 education — through curriculum restriction laws, book ban campaigns, attacks on teachers in political rhetoric, and legislative scrutiny of classroom content — has become an increasingly documented driver of teacher attrition and recruitment deterrence [11][23].

**Florida case study:** Florida has experienced an acute and well-documented deterioration:
- **31% of Florida teachers have fewer than four years of experience** — a figure that reflects both aggressive turnover among experienced teachers and the normalization of underprepared entrants [23].
- The use of temporary or out-of-field teachers has doubled in five years [23].
- The Florida Education Association attributes the crisis in part to "micromanagement from legislators, excessive regulations, and Florida's 50th-ranked teacher pay" [23].
- Survey and interview data from UC San Diego and NYU Steinhardt found that teachers across Florida reported direct experience with restriction-related censorship pressure and described a "culture of fear" directly linked to their intentions to leave [23].

**National surveys:** RAND's 2024 survey found that political and societal challenges to teaching — including attacks on educator credibility and legislative restrictions on classroom content — were cited by a meaningful share of teachers as contributing to their stress and intention to leave, though the survey does not isolate this as a quantified independent cause [5].

## Part 4: Policy Solutions — What the Evidence Supports

### 4.1 Substantial Pay Increases

Evidence strongly supports that **large, sustained pay increases reduce teacher turnover** and modestly improve recruitment. Small or symbolic raises have limited effect. Key findings:

**Arkansas LEARNS Act (2023):** Arkansas raised its minimum teacher salary from $36,000 to $50,000 and mandated a minimum $2,000 raise for all teachers. A University of Arkansas study found that teachers receiving the largest salary increases were significantly more likely to remain in their districts, with the strongest retention effects in rural and high-poverty districts where starting salaries historically lagged most [29].

**Washington State post-McCleary reforms:** Following a state supreme court mandate to fully fund public education, Washington overhauled its funding formula starting in 2018, increasing average teacher salaries by approximately **$18,000** statewide. Teacher turnover dropped by **3–5 percentage points** in the following years, with particularly strong effects for teachers with eight or more years of experience [29][30].

**National school finance reform analysis:** An analysis of school finance reforms across states found that average raises of approximately $4,000 were associated with a **3-percentage-point reduction in teacher turnover**, with stronger effects in high-poverty and high-minority districts [29].

**RAND (2024):** Survey data confirm that teachers who receive larger pay increases are less likely to report intentions to leave. The study estimates that raises in the $16,000–17,000 range would shift most teachers from reporting their pay as "inadequate" to "adequate" [5].

*Policy implication:* As a matter of empirical record, pay increases work — but they must be large enough to be meaningful. At least 23 states proposed or enacted salary increases in 2023; the evidence suggests that only those making substantial, system-level investments will see meaningful retention and recruitment improvements [29].

### 4.2 Student Loan Forgiveness — PSLF and State Programs

**Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF)** offers loan cancellation to public school teachers who make 120 qualifying monthly payments under an income-driven repayment plan while employed full-time by a qualifying public school. Teachers employed by nonprofit charter schools may also qualify.

**Recent improvements:** Following administrative reforms beginning in 2021, the program became dramatically more accessible. As of May 2024, nearly **876,000 public service workers** — including many teachers — had received loan forgiveness under PSLF, compared to just approximately 7,000 before reform [31].

**SAVE Plan (launched 2023):** The Saving on a Valuable Education income-driven repayment plan, combined with PSLF, significantly reduces total lifetime payments. A teacher with a typical bachelor's degree debt of approximately $32,000 would pay approximately **$6,800 under SAVE + PSLF**, compared to $20,000–24,000 under prior IDR plans [31][32].

*Note: As of the date of this brief, the SAVE plan is subject to ongoing federal litigation, and its long-term status is uncertain. Congress may act to modify or eliminate it. Researchers and advocates should monitor ongoing judicial and legislative developments.*

**Remaining gaps:** Effective participation requires active enrollment management. FFEL and Perkins loans must be consolidated into Direct Loans; Parent PLUS loans face deadline constraints for consolidation. Prior to reforms, poor program administration and servicer errors resulted in many eligible borrowers being wrongly denied [31].

**State loan forgiveness programs:** Many states operate separate teacher loan forgiveness programs, particularly for shortage areas and high-need schools. Coverage and benefits vary significantly [VERIFY: comprehensive 50-state comparison of state teacher loan programs, most recent year].

### 4.3 Teacher Residency Programs

Teacher residency programs model teacher preparation on medical residencies — year-long, paid clinical placements in schools, with residents working alongside expert mentor teachers while completing coordinated coursework.

**Evidence base (Learning Policy Institute):** Residency-prepared teachers demonstrate [33][34]:
- **Higher retention rates** than traditionally prepared or alternatively certified teachers, reducing district turnover costs
- **Better preparedness** for the realities of teaching, including classroom management and culturally responsive instruction
- **Greater diversity** — residencies have been particularly effective at recruiting and retaining teachers of color, and are often designed to address specific subject-area shortages
- Strongest outcomes when programs include: tight integration of coursework with clinical practice, strong district-university partnerships, robust mentoring systems, and financial stipends that offset the cost of the preparation year in exchange for a multi-year service commitment to the district

**Funding mechanisms:** Residencies are more expensive than traditional preparation programs, requiring investment from districts, universities, and state/federal grants (e.g., Teacher Quality Partnership Grants under Title II). The up-front cost is offset over time by reduced turnover and hiring expenses [33][34].

### 4.4 Housing Assistance and Cost-of-Living Supplements

In high-cost housing markets, salary increases alone may be insufficient to retain teachers. Several interventions show promise:

- **Teacher Next Door Program:** Offers housing grants of up to $9,000 and down payment assistance of up to $24,000 for eligible teachers [35].
- **Educator workforce housing:** California, Colorado, and Ohio have begun developing subsidized housing on school district-owned land. California's rent burden crisis — affecting one-third of school employees — makes this a recognized emergency rather than a marginal benefit [17][35].
- **Illinois Teacher Vacancy Grant Pilot Program:** Gives districts flexibility to use funds for housing stipends, down payment assistance, or signing bonuses. Approximately 60% of awards go to rural districts [35].
- **Rural Education Investment Act (proposed):** Would direct Teacher Quality Partnership Grant funds proportionally to rural areas, supporting residency programs with cost-of-living stipend components [35].

### 4.5 Reducing Administrative Burden and Improving Working Conditions

Working condition reforms do not require legislative action at scale and can be implemented at the district level, yet they address documented root causes of attrition. Evidence-supported strategies include [5][6]:

- **Reducing meeting load and non-instructional tasks:** Time studies consistently find teachers spending substantial hours on bureaucratic compliance, redundant paperwork, and low-value meetings. Auditing and eliminating unnecessary administrative requirements is an evidence-aligned first step.
- **Providing planning time:** Adequate protected planning periods are associated with teacher job satisfaction and retention.
- **Improving principal leadership quality:** Research identifies principal support as one of the most powerful predictors of teacher retention at the school level. Instructional leadership training for principals has measurable effects.
- **Trauma-informed training and classroom support:** Teachers are more likely to stay when they feel equipped to address student mental health and behavioral challenges. This requires investment in school counselors, social workers, and specialized training — not simply expecting teachers to absorb unlimited additional responsibilities.

### 4.6 Restoring and Strengthening Collective Bargaining Rights

The evidence linking collective bargaining to better teacher compensation is robust. States with statutory bargaining rights pay teachers 24–26% more on average [3][9]. Specific policy directions:

- **State legislation:** States without collective bargaining statutes can enact them; states with weak or fragmented laws can strengthen them.
- **Repeal of restrictive legislation:** Wisconsin's Act 10 and similar laws have demonstrably increased turnover and suppressed wages. Repeal or amendment would restore teacher voice and competitiveness.
- **Scope of bargaining:** States that restrict the subjects of bargaining (as Indiana does) can expand bargaining to include class size, planning time, and working conditions — all of which directly affect teacher quality and retention.
- **Janus remediation:** Congress could establish a framework ensuring public employees can effectively organize and fund collective action in the post-*Janus* environment [8][9][21].

### 4.7 Protecting Academic Freedom — Model Legislation

Protecting teachers from content-based censorship requires affirmative legal frameworks at both state and federal levels. Policy dimensions include:

- **State repeal of "educational gag order" laws:** The 47 enacted laws restricting instruction on race, gender, and LGBTQ topics are documented drivers of teacher attrition. Repeal would directly address a documented chilling effect [11][23].
- **Whistleblower protection:** Strengthening retaliation protections for teachers who report violations, speak publicly on education policy, or resist pressure to violate students' educational rights.
- **Affirmative academic freedom statutes:** Several state legislatures have considered bills that would provide teachers with statutory protection for pedagogical decisions within recognized professional standards.
- **Federal model:** A federal Academic Freedom in K–12 Education Act (no current statute) could establish floor protections for teacher professional judgment against politically motivated legislative overreach.

### 4.8 Diversifying the Teacher Pipeline

The persistent demographic gap between teachers and students demands sustained, multi-strategy investment.

**Grow-your-own (GYO) programs** recruit future teachers from local communities — often current paraprofessionals, community members, or recent high school graduates — and provide pathways to full certification while remaining in their home communities. Key evidence [33][36]:

- A California paraprofessional GYO pathway documented a **92% long-term retention rate** among completers — far above the rates typical of traditional teacher preparation programs.
- GYO completers are more likely to reflect the cultural and linguistic backgrounds of students in high-need schools.
- Effective GYO pathways for paraprofessionals require: flexible scheduling, financial support (particularly for the certification exam and credential coursework), cohort models for peer support, advising, and targeted test preparation.

**Paraprofessional pathways** are particularly promising as paraprofessionals already possess school experience, community ties, and language skills that the teacher workforce needs. Many states have streamlined or begun streamlining the paraprofessional-to-teacher pathway, though inconsistent funding limits scale [36].

**Diversification of the teaching profession** must address both recruitment (who enters teacher preparation) and retention (who stays in the profession). Targeted scholarships, mentorship programs for teachers of color, and targeted interventions in schools where teachers of color face higher attrition are all evidence-informed strategies [12][25].

### 4.9 Class Size Reduction

The Tennessee STAR (Student-Teacher Achievement Ratio) study (1985–1989) remains the most rigorous randomized experiment on class size in K–12 education. Its findings, subsequently replicated and extended [37][38]:

- Students randomly assigned to classes of **13–17 students** significantly outperformed those in regular classes (22–26 students) on standardized tests across all demographic groups and school settings (urban, suburban, rural).
- Benefits persisted into later grades, even after students returned to larger classes. Students who spent three or more years in small classes were significantly more likely to graduate high school, take college entrance exams, and graduate in the top 10% of their class.
- The Black–white achievement gap was reduced by as much as **56%** for students who began school in small classes.
- Tennessee's Project Challenge subsequently implemented small classes in the state's poorest districts, raising those districts from near the bottom to near the middle of state performance rankings.

**Implications for teacher retention:** While STAR did not directly measure teacher retention, teachers in smaller classes reported fewer discipline problems, greater instructional effectiveness, and better working conditions — all of which are associated with lower attrition in the broader literature. Class size reduction also addresses a root cause of burnout: the cognitive and emotional demands of managing 30+ students simultaneously [37][38].

*Caution:* Class size reduction is expensive and requires a commensurate increase in the number of qualified teachers. In a context of shortage, reducing class sizes without first addressing pipeline and compensation may create additional hiring pressure. The policy is most effective as part of a package that addresses supply simultaneously.

### 4.10 Mental Health Support for Educators

Burnout rates among teachers (53–60%) are approximately double those of comparable working adults [5][6]. This is both a humanitarian crisis and a strategic policy challenge — burned-out teachers are far more likely to leave, and their effectiveness is compromised while they remain.

Evidence-supported investments include:
- **Employee Assistance Programs (EAPs)** accessible to all school staff, fully covered, and actively promoted rather than left as obscure HR benefits
- **Reduced caseloads and protected planning time** to address workload as a structural driver
- **Peer support networks and mentorship programs**, particularly for early-career teachers who face the highest risk of burnout and attrition in the first three years
- **School counselor and social worker ratios:** The American School Counselor Association recommends a maximum of 250 students per counselor; most schools far exceed this ratio, placing additional mental health burden on teachers. ## Part 5: Equity Dimensions

### 5.1 Teacher Diversity vs. Student Population Demographics

The United States has a profound demographic mismatch between its student body and its teaching workforce:

| Group | Share of Students | Share of Teachers |
|-------|------------------|------------------|
| White | ~49% | ~75% |
| Black or African American | ~16% | ~10% |
| Hispanic or Latino | ~26% | ~11% |
| Asian American Pacific Islander | ~7.5% | ~3.7% |

Source: NCES Common Core of Data, 2022–23; TNTP analysis [12][39].

In 2022–23, **52% of public school students were students of color, but only 21–22% of teachers were people of color**. The representation gap — the difference between student and teacher diversity — has barely moved over the prior decade [12][39].

This gap matters for documented educational reasons:
- Research consistently finds that students of color demonstrate **better academic outcomes, lower exclusionary discipline rates, and higher educational aspirations** when taught by a same-race teacher, controlling for other factors [12][25].
- The effect is particularly well-documented for Black students assigned to Black teachers: one study found that Black students in grades 3–5 assigned to a Black teacher for even one year were more likely to graduate high school and enroll in college [VERIFY: cite original study — Gershenson et al. or similar, verify publication details].

### 5.2 Pay Disparities by Race and Gender Within Teaching

Despite standardized salary schedules intended to minimize pay discrimination, pay disparities by race and gender persist within the teaching profession:

**Gender:** Research using the National Teacher and Principal Survey found that male teachers receive, on average, approximately $700 more in base pay and $1,500 more in supplemental compensation (coaching, extra duties) than female teachers with similar experience and credentials — yielding an annual gap of approximately **$4,000** when all compensation is included, equivalent to a 7% pay disadvantage for women [40]. The Brookings Institution confirms this pattern in its analysis of gender pay gaps in K–12 education [40].

This is paradoxical in a workforce that is approximately 76% female. The gap partly reflects the differential allocation of supplemental assignments (e.g., coaching) that are paid separately and tend to go to men, particularly when principals are male.

**Race:** RAND's 2024 State of the American Teacher Survey found that Black teachers earned an average base salary of approximately **$65,013**, while White teachers earned approximately **$70,695** — a gap of over $5,600. White teachers also received larger annual raises on average ($2,030 vs. $1,684 for Black teachers) [41][42].

- Black teachers are more likely to report that low pay is a primary source of job-related stress, and are disproportionately assigned to high-need schools where pay often lags [41].
- Black female teachers face intersecting disadvantages: they are among the lowest-paid groups in the teaching workforce, reflecting the compounding effects of both gender and racial pay disparities [40][42].

**Collective bargaining reduces these gaps.** Research finds that unions and collective bargaining agreements reduce gender and racial pay disparities within teaching by introducing objective salary standards, limiting managerial discretion in pay determination, and expanding benefits that disproportionately benefit women and workers of color [9][21].

### 5.3 Resource Inequities Between High-Poverty and Wealthy Districts

School funding in the United States remains substantially tied to local property tax wealth, creating systematic resource inequities that fall disproportionately on high-poverty districts — which are also disproportionately comprised of students of color. In the context of teacher quality:

- **Experience and qualification gaps:** Research consistently finds that students in high-poverty districts are **less likely to be taught by experienced, fully certified teachers** than students in affluent districts, both across and within districts [25][43].
- **Within-district inequities:** Even within a single district, high-poverty schools often receive less experienced teachers than low-poverty schools in the same district, driven by seniority-based transfer rights and the revealed preferences of experienced teachers for more stable working environments [43].
- **NCES vacancy data:** 57% of high-poverty schools reported vacancies in fall 2022, compared to lower rates for more affluent schools [25]. High-poverty schools also rely more heavily on long-term substitutes and out-of-field teachers.

### 5.4 How Shortages Fall Disproportionately on High-Need Schools

The concentration of teacher shortages in high-need schools is not random — it reflects and reinforces systemic inequality. Students in the schools with the greatest academic need receive the least experienced and least stable teacher workforces. The implications for equity are well-documented:

- **Access to experienced teachers correlates with student outcomes.** Students with access to experienced, well-matched teachers demonstrate better academic growth. Concentrating turnover and vacancy in high-need schools actively widens the achievement gap [25][43].
- **Teachers of color disproportionately leave high-need schools.** Despite being more likely to teach in high-need schools, teachers of color face higher attrition in those settings — driven by lower pay, more challenging conditions, and inadequate administrative support. Retention rates for teachers of color in New York were **50%** overall (vs. 64% for White teachers), with Black and Native American teachers at approximately 45% [44].
- **The U.S. Commission on Civil Rights** has characterized teacher shortages in high-poverty and high-minority schools as a civil rights issue, noting that they violate students' equal access to educational opportunity [24][25].

## Part 6: Policy Recommendations Summary

Based on the evidence reviewed in this brief, the Freedom & Dignity Project supports the following policy directions:

1. **Raise teacher pay substantially and sustainably** — targeting a minimum starting salary of $60,000 nationally and closing the wage penalty through state and federal investment. Evidence shows large raises reduce turnover; small ones do not.
2. **Restore and strengthen collective bargaining rights** for public school teachers in all states, including repeal of Act 10-style restrictions and legislation nullified by *Janus*.
3. **Repeal educational gag order laws** — the 47 enacted censorship statutes are documented drivers of teacher attrition and represent unconstitutional restrictions on professional judgment.
4. **Expand and fund teacher residency programs** and grow-your-own paraprofessional pipelines, with priority for high-need subjects and high-need communities, and explicit goals for diversifying the workforce.
5. **Strengthen and make permanent PSLF** for teachers in public and qualifying nonprofit schools; create parallel state loan forgiveness programs with clear and navigable enrollment procedures.
6. **Address the housing crisis** facing teachers in high-cost markets through federal and state workforce housing investment, down payment assistance, and educator-specific cost-of-living supplements.
7. **Reduce class sizes** — particularly in early elementary grades and in high-poverty schools — with accompanying investments in the teacher pipeline to ensure qualified teachers are available.
8. **Invest in educator mental health** — through EAPs, peer support, reduced administrative burden, and aligned investment in school counselors, social workers, and mental health supports for students.
9. **Close the teacher diversity gap** through targeted recruitment, financial support, and retention investments — recognizing that diverse teachers improve outcomes for students of color.
10. **Audit and address pay inequities** by race and gender within teaching through salary transparency requirements, pay equity audits, and expanded collective bargaining coverage.
